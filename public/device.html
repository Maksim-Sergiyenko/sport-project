device: "It was good for me to have a reset"

The Danish AWPer opened up about the challenging end to his Astralis stint and the expectations for the start of the season with 100 Thieves. The newly built 100 Thieves roster is off to a promising start, jumping to 36th in the global VRS off the back of a quarter-final run at Roman Imperium Cup V and a second-place finish at DraculaN Season 5.

One of the main questions when the team was created revolved around Nicolai "⁠device⁠" Reedtz, who endured a frustrating 2025 season with a struggling Astralis lineup. The veteran AWPer, who averaged a 1.04 rating on LAN last year, said that he knew in the summer that "it would be my last season with Astralis."

"It was not the same, for sure," he told HLTV before the team's LAN debut at Roman Imperium Cup. "I think I was a part of it as well.

"It was a hard environment for a lot of people to stay motivated. You can see that a lot of really competent players are not the same version of themselves when they play in Astralis.

"Hopefully, the new owners can change that." 100 Thieves find themselves grinding tier-2 LANs to climb the rankings and get into a position where they can earn invites to bigger tournaments.

It's far from the glamorous world one would envision for storied players like device and Håvard "⁠rain⁠" Nygaard, but the Danish AWPer said he is relishing this fresh start.

"No matter if we go to the best LANs in the world or these LANs, the desire to win is still there, the motivation to win is still there," he noted.

"For me, it was good to have a reset as well, with going down the ladder in the competition and also building myself up with the, let's say, self-perception. I think I was in a bad place in Astralis in the end. Here, there has been a good timeline to get back to the level I used to be at.

"I think that when you go to the big tournaments with a team like Astralis or FaZe, when they were struggling as well, you're facing the top-one team in the first game, and you're on the lower bracket instantly. It's a bit of a different vibe here. I think a lot of it is more down to energy, motivation and hunger as well.

"But we are also a new team with five players who have never played together before, and a new in-game leader and a new coach. So there are a lot of things [where we] need to find a balance. That requires a lot of games, especially LANs, to really play to win and see how things are working out." With that in mind, expectations have to be tempered. There will be moments when the team will hit a wall — like the online defeat to MOUZ NXT in the open qualifier for IEM Atlanta and the 13-2 thrashing at the hands of Ninjas in Pyjamas in their first LAN match. That's why, device noted, it's critical to be process-driven, rather than results-driven, during this early stage.

"I  think that the most important thing is seeing the progress within the team and in the dynamics on the micro, on the map and stuff like this," he said.

"If things click like they do some days in practice, we can be very good. There's not like a certain goal, short-term, for sure."
